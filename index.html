The Hospital Throughput Problem
The most common operational question in every hospital: "Why is there an empty room while patients are waiting in the ED?"
Without RRT: Manual Coordination
How discharge-to-admit actually works today — bed status is inferred, not measured
  • Patient physically leaves — but the bed stays "occupied" until the nurse completes discharge documentation in Epic
  • There is often a lag between physical discharge and documentation, because nurses prioritize patients still on the unit over the one who has left.
  • Discharge documentation changes room status from occupied → soiled — that status change is what triggers the EVS request
  • EVS dispatches based on their own prioritization and staff availability — this is a terminal clean (full surface wipe-down), not a daily clean (trash, basics)
  • Patient placement — the nurse doing facility-wide bed assignment — watches the soiled → in-progress → clean status and can assign the next patient even before the clean is fully finished
  • Transport is triggered once the room is clean and a patient is assigned — not automatically
  • Nurses aren't watching the idle room — an empty bed means more incoming work, so there's a mild disincentive to rush documentation
  • Bed status is inferred throughout — either from manual charting or by assuming the patient left when the discharge order was signed
With RRT: Automated Workflow Orchestration
Kontakt.io automates every handoff — nothing is manual, nothing is missed
  • Nanotag IR wristband detects patient departure → Kontakt.io fires soiled-room signal within seconds — no nurse documentation required
  • Epic's built-in RTLS Interface receives the signal and alerts EVS automatically — 3-week Epic build, then fully hands-off for Epic customers
  • EVS receives a room-specific mobile work request instantly — no phone call, no rounding to discover dirty rooms
  • Room cleaned → Epic updates room status in real time — Patient Placement sees it the second it's done, and can assign the next patient immediately
  • Transport dispatched in parallel with EVS cleaning (Stage 2) — arrives as room clears, cutting sequential wait time
  • New patient assigned and moved to a verified-ready room — entire cycle shortened by 90 minutes vs. status quo
  • Every handoff timestamped, auditable, and measurable — ROI evidence built automatically, no manual reporting

The Automated Workflow — Step by Step
From Epic discharge to new patient occupying the room — with zero manual coordination steps.
1

Attending Writes Discharge Order — Clinical Process Begins

The physician signs the discharge order in Epic, starting the clinical discharge process. The bedside nurse begins discharge education: medications, follow-up appointments, and instructions. This typically takes 1–2 hours of appropriate clinical time. During this window the patient is preparing to leave — this education period is not the problem being solved. The bottleneck comes after the patient physically departs.

2

RTLS Detects Patient Departure — Where the Bottleneck Is Eliminated

When the patient's Nanotag IR wristband exits the room, Kontakt.io detects physical departure within seconds — without waiting for nurse documentation. This is where the 90-minute bottleneck is actually eliminated. A configurable 15-minute buffer can be built in to confirm the patient is truly gone before the soiled-room signal fires. Departure can also be triggered by geofence entry — for example, when a patient enters a discharge lounge — as an alternative signal for cases where they won't return to the room. For Epic customers, this RTLS data triggers Epic's built-in RTLS Interface to mark the room soiled. Nurses still complete documentation, but it is no longer a bottleneck.

3

EVS Receives Automated Work Request — Epic-Native Step

Once RTLS fires the soiled-room signal, Epic's built-in RTLS Interface dispatches the EVS work request — this is a native Epic workflow, not a Kontakt.io feature. For Epic customers, Kontakt.io does not touch this step: the EHR handles EVS notification directly. The EVS team receives a room-specific alert and performs a terminal clean (full surface wipe-down). No phone call. No rounding to find dirty rooms. No discovery delay.

4

Parallel Transport Dispatch

This is not a native Epic workflow — it is a Kontakt.io add-on layered on top. It requires patients to be assigned to beds that are dirty or in-progress, and EVS staff to be badged. When those conditions are met, Kontakt.io dispatches transport in parallel with cleaning — so the next patient is en route as the room clears. This saves approximately ~15 minutes per discharge, on top of the benefit from Step 3 — not split with it. Seller note: Stage 2 is sold but rarely activated in practice — Stage 1 already delivers the primary benefit.

5

Room Status Updates — Inside Epic in Real Time

When EVS marks the room clean, Epic's room status updates immediately. For Epic customers, the entire process runs inside Epic — there is no separate Kontakt.io interface for frontline staff. Patient Placement, charge nurses, and Command Center all see the bed become available the instant it is — not 60 minutes later. No manual check-ins required.

6

Tag Compliance Email — Daily System Health Check

Each morning, a daily email goes to relevant staff listing every patient admitted the prior night who either (a) does not have a tag assigned, or (b) has a tag assigned but with a dead battery or other malfunction. This daily loop ensures tags stay functional and every patient is properly tracked — without which the entire automated workflow breaks down silently. This step is often underemphasized during implementations but is critical to sustained accuracy.

7

Patient Assigned — Room Occupied — Data Captured

With real-time bed visibility, Patient Placement assigns the next patient immediately. The new patient is transported to a verified-ready room. Every timestamp is captured — vacancy detected, EVS alert sent, cleaning complete, patient assigned — giving leadership a full audit trail of every discharge cycle.

Step 2 — The Technology

Portal Beam: Thermal Imaging, No Patient Tags

The Portal Beam mounts on the exam room ceiling and takes thermal images to count heat signatures — it knows how many people are in the room, but retains no actual image and stores no PHI. Because staff and providers wear BLE badges, Access Agent deduces: 2 heat signatures + 1 known provider badge = 1 patient present. No patient tagging needed. This creates a full visit timeline (rooming start, patient-alone wait, provider time, turnaround) — data Epic has never had.

Infrastructure: The Portal Beam handles thermal imaging; the Beam Mini serves as the location engine. Two deployment configurations: one with no cabling required, and a PoE single-device option.

Competitive differentiator: Kontakt.io is the only vendor offering a tagless approach. In typical MOB settings with multiple encounters per day, tagless is dramatically simpler to operate than tagged systems (like Midmark) that require patient tags to be placed and removed on every visit.

1. By 2030, what percentage of health system revenue is projected to come from ambulatory care — up from just 30% today?

Correct. By 2030, analysts project ambulatory will generate 70% of health system revenue — flipping from today's ~30%. This is why every boardroom conversation is now about ambulatory.

2. What is the current average exam room utilization at most outpatient clinics — and why is it so low?

Correct. 33% — meaning 2 of every 3 room-hours go completely unused. Three layers compound to produce this: the 2:1 room ratio sets a 50% structural ceiling, provider schedule variability drops it to ~40%, and scheduling template constraints take it the rest of the way to 33%. Access Agent addresses all three layers.

3. How does Access Agent measure real-time room occupancy in ambulatory clinics — without requiring patients to wear a tag or badge?

Correct. The Portal Beam mounts on the ceiling and takes thermal images to count heat signatures — it knows how many people are in the room, but retains no actual image. Because staff and providers wear badges, Access Agent can deduce: 2 people in the room + 1 is a known provider = the other is a patient. No patient tagging required. This is a key differentiator from Midmark, which requires patients to wear tags.

4. Access Agent operates in 3 stages. Which stage delivers 70% of the total revenue opportunity — and why is it the biggest lever?

Correct. Dynamic Rooming accounts for 70% of the total addressable opportunity — $90.7M for an 800-room system. Instead of every provider permanently owning 2 rooms, rooms are shared dynamically across a zone of providers (e.g., 6 rooms for 4 providers = 1.5:1). This moves the utilization ceiling from 50% to ~80%. It's the biggest lever because it addresses the structural constraint — not just a workflow improvement.

5. A CNIO says: "My clinical staff are already overwhelmed — I can't add another tool for them to learn." What's your best response?

Correct. This is a key differentiator. Access Agent uses Epic's Galaxy checklist integration, so clinical staff (nurses, MAs, providers) never see a new tool, a new alert, or a new login. The AI recommendations surface exclusively to ops coordinators and practice managers in their existing workflow. The intelligence is invisible to clinical staff — it works in the background.
